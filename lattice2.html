Solving lattice models — Part 2

Following from the last blog, I want now to introduce the concept of a lattice containing two kind of atoms. One way of doing that is to consider a linear chain with atoms.

Following the notation of the last blog, we can describe such system by a Hamiltonian under the approximation for second-neighbors.

\begin{align} H = -\mu\sum_{i}^{N} \left( a_{\sigma,i}^{\dagger} a_{\sigma,i} + b_{\sigma,i}^{\dagger} b_{\sigma,i}\right) + -\frac{t_1}{2} \sum_{i}^{N} \left(b_{\sigma,i+1}^{\dagger} a_{\sigma,i} +\mbox{h.c.} \right) + -\frac{t_2}{2}\sum_{i}^{N-1} a_{\sigma,i+2}^{\dagger} a_{\sigma,i} + b_{\sigma,i+2}^{\dagger} b_{\sigma,i} + \mbox{h.c.} \end{align}

Considering now a closed system, we can use the Fourier transform to change to momentum space, remember that this simplified the calculation in the previous system. The calculations are exactly the same as in the previous blog post, except for the second-neighboring term

\begin{align} \sum_{i}^{N} b_{\sigma,i+1}^{\dagger} a_{\sigma,i} = \sum_{i}^{N} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k,k'} e^{- i k a} e^{-i (k-k') x_i} b_{\sigma,k}^{\dagger} a_{\sigma,k'} = \sum_{k} e^{- i k a} b_{\sigma,k}^{\dagger} a_{\sigma,k} \end{align} \begin{align} \sum_{i}^{N-1} c_{\sigma,i+2}^{\dagger}c_{\sigma,i} = \sum_{i}^{N-1} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k} \sum_{k'} e^{- i k 2 a} e^{-i (k-k') x_i} c_{\sigma,k}^{\dagger} c_{\sigma,k'} = \sum_{k} e^{- i k 2 a} c_{\sigma,k}^{\dagger} c_{\sigma,k} \qquad c = a, b \end{align}

Therefore we can write the Hamiltonian as

\begin{align} H = \sum_{k} (\mu - t_2 \cos k 2 a) a_{\sigma,k}^{\dagger} a_{\sigma,k} + (\mu - t_2 \cos k 2 a) b_{\sigma,k}^{\dagger} b_{\sigma,k} + t_1 e^{- i k a} b_{\sigma,k}^{\dagger}a_{\sigma,k} + t_1 e^{i k a} a_{\sigma,k}^{\dagger} b_{\sigma,k} \end{align}

We see now that the introduction of another ´kind´ of atom in the chain translates to an off-diagonal term after Fourier transforming the Hamiltonian.

\begin{align} H = \left(\begin{array}{rrrr} -t_{2} \cos\left(a k\right) + \mu & t_{1} e^{\left(\frac{1}{2} i \, a k\right)} & 0 & 0 & \dots \\ t_{1} e^{\left(-\frac{1}{2} i \, a k\right)} & -t_{2} \cos\left(a k\right) + \mu & t_{1} e^{\left(\frac{1}{2} i \, a k\right)} & 0 & \dots \\ 0 & t_{1} e^{\left(-\frac{1}{2} i \, a k\right)} & -t_{2} \cos\left(a k\right) + \mu & t_{1} e^{\left(\frac{1}{2} i \, a k\right)} & \dots \\ 0 & 0 & t_{1} e^{\left(-\frac{1}{2} i \, a k\right)} & -t_{2} \cos\left(a k\right) + \mu & \dots \\ \dots & \dots & \dots & \dots & \dots \end{array}\right) \end{align}

If you take a closer look at the matricial form of the Hamiltonian above, you will notice that while it is not in diagonal form anymore, it is almost-diagonal, meaning that it is block-diagonal.

To understand what is happening we can proceed in the following way. For now we will let \(\mu = 0\) and \(t_2 = a = 1\) and \(t_1 = t\), we can define the following objects

\begin{align} H' = \left(\begin{array}{rr} -\cos\left(k\right) & te^{\left(\frac{1}{2} i \,k\right)}\\ t e^{\left(-\frac{1}{2} i \,k\right)} & -\cos\left(a k\right)\\ \end{array}\right) \qquad T' = \left(\begin{array}{rr} 0 & 0\\ t e^{\left(-\frac{1}{2} i \,k\right)} & 0\\ \end{array}\right) \end{align}

and rewrite the Hamiltonian in the following way

\begin{align} H = \left(\begin{array}{rrrrr} H' & T' & 0 & 0 & \dots\\ T'^{\dagger} & H' & 0 & 0 & \dots\\ \dots & \dots& \dots& \dots& \dots\\ \end{array}\right) \end{align}

Then we can perform the following operation. First we eigendecompose \(H'\) such that \(H' = O H'' O^{\dagger}\) where \(H''\) is now a diagonal matrix. Then, in this basis, \(T' = O^{\dagger} T'' O\) and we can rewrite the Hamiltonian as

\begin{align} H = \left(\begin{array}{rr} H'' & T'' & 0 & 0 & \dots\\ T''^{\dagger} & H'' & 0 & 0 & \dots\\ \dots & \dots& \dots& \dots& \dots\\ \end{array}\right) \end{align}

That is

\begin{align} H'' = \left(\begin{array}{rr} -t - \cos\left(k\right) & 0 \\ 0 & t - \cos\left(k\right)\\ \end{array}\right) \qquad O = \left(\begin{array}{rr} 1 & 1 \\ -e^{\left(-\frac{1}{2} i \, k\right)} & e^{\left(-\frac{1}{2} i \, k\right)}\\ \end{array}\right) \qquad T'' = \left(\begin{array}{rr} -t & -t \\ t & t\\ \end{array}\right) \end{align}

As a crude approximation, we can assume that \(T'' = 0\) and see that the effect of \(t\) is to change the eigenvalues around \(-\cos k\) by \(\pm t\). Now by time-independent perturbation theory we can find the first-order correction to the eigenvalues

\begin{align} E_0 = \bra{\psi_0} T'' \ket{\psi_0} = \left( \begin{array}{cc} 1,\,-e^{\left(\frac{1}{2} i \, k\right)} \end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{rr} t e^{\left(-\frac{1}{2} i \, k\right)} & -t \\ t e^{\left(-i \, k\right)} & -t e^{\left(-\frac{1}{2} i \, k\right)}\\ \end{array}\right) \left( \begin{array}{cc} 1 \\ -e^{\left(-\frac{1}{2} i \, k\right)}\\ \end{array}\right) = 0\\ E_1 = \bra{\psi_1} T'' \ket{\psi_1} = \left( \begin{array}{cc} 1,\,e^{\left(\frac{1}{2} i \, k\right)} \end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{rr} t e^{\left(-\frac{1}{2} i \, k\right)} & -t \\ t e^{\left(-i \, k\right)} & -t e^{\left(-\frac{1}{2} i \, k\right)}\\ \end{array}\right) \left( \begin{array}{cc} 1 \\ e^{\left(-\frac{1}{2} i \, k\right)}\\ \end{array}\right) = 0 \end{align}

This calculation also justifies our previous assumption of neglecting \(T''\), we see that up to first-order it does not make any difference to keep it or not.
